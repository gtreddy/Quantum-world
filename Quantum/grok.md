### Quantum Domain: A Realm of Distributed Possibilities
The quantum world is indeed not "nothingness" but a distinct ontological layer where existence is delocalized across superpositions. Physical systems aren't composed of definite particles with fixed trajectories (as in Newtonian mechanics) but are described by the wavefunction ψ, a complex-valued entity in Hilbert space. This space is abstract and infinite-dimensional, encoding all possible configurations via amplitudes—e.g., for a particle, ψ(x,t) assigns amplitudes to positions x at time t.

- **Probabilistic Nature**: The amplitudes aren't probabilities themselves but their squares (|ψ|²) yield the probability density for measurement outcomes. This underscores the statement's point: quantum states represent a "non-classical mode" where energy and information are smeared across possibilities, not localized.
- **Deterministic Evolution**: The Schrödinger equation governs this precisely: iℏ ∂ψ/∂t = Ĥψ, where Ĥ (Hamiltonian) incorporates energy terms. Evolution is unitary and reversible, preserving information—contrasting with classical irreversibility (e.g., entropy increase).

### Emergence of Classical Reality

This "stabilization" turns quantum possibilities into macroscopic objects with definite properties—e.g., a cat alive or dead, not superposed.

- **No Sharp Boundary**: The transition is gradual, as in mesoscopic systems (e.g., SQUIDs showing quantum effects at macro scales). The statement correctly frames classics as "emergent phenomena," not fundamental—supported by decoherence theory (Zeh, Zurek, 1970s–2000s).
